Flint, Michigan: Water Vulnerability

The Flint water crisis highlighted the urgent need to identify communities at greatest risk for water contamination. This spatial analysis addresses the critical question: who is most vulnerable to drinking poisonous tap water in Flint? Using data from the University of Michigan's Toxic Release Inventory and municipal infrastructure records, I created a multi-criteria vulnerability assessment that identifies high-risk areas based on three overlapping factors: proximity to TRI facilities with water pollution exceeding 10,000 tons, distance to rivers that could carry contaminants, and the presence of lead service lines within 0.2 miles. The resulting map reveals concentrated zones of vulnerability where all three risk factors converge, enabling targeted intervention strategies and resource allocation for affected residents.

Skills Used:
  • XY Table to Point Tool - converted latitude and longitude values into spatial points for TRI facilities
  • Project Tool - re-projected TRI facility points into Michigan South StatePlane coordinate system
  • Join Tool - attached water pollution emission data to TRI facility points using TRI_ID field
  • Select By Attributes Tool - filtered TRI facilities and isolated lead service lines
  • Buffer Tool - created dissolved proximity zones around TRI facilities, rivers, and lead service lines
  • Intersect Tool - identified overlapping areas where all three risk criteria were met
  • Calculate Geometry Tool - measured the area in square feet of each high-risk polygon

Miami Population Density

When comparing population density maps at the Census Tract and Block levels, two key spatial analysis problems arise: the ecological fallacy and the modifiable areal unit problem (MAUP). The ecological fallacy occurs when inferences are drawn about individuals from aggregate data. For example, a tract-level map might show high population density, but that doesn't mean every part of the tract is densely populated or that every resident experiences crowding. The tract average masks internal variation, and drawing conclusions about individuals from this generalized data can lead to misleading interpretations. MAUP, on the other hand, refers to how statistical results can change depending on the spatial units used. Census Tracts are larger and more generalized, while Blocks are much smaller and more granular. When mapping population density, the tract-level map smooths out local variability, potentially hiding pockets of extreme density or sparsity. The block-level map reveals these patterns but creates noise and complexity.

Skills Used:
  • Project Tool - altered data from an incorrect projection to a geographically appropriate projection
  • Joins and Relates - combined data using identical fields to create comprehensive datasets
  • Field Calculator - created formulas to generate previously unavailable data
  • Calculate Area - provided geographic data missing from the initial dataset

Chicago Homicide Density

This choropleth map examines the spatial distribution of homicide density across Chicago's police districts to understand how violent crime patterns might impact urban tourism and economic development. Using crime incident data from the City of Chicago Data Portal, I calculated homicide density per square mile for each police district, revealing stark geographic disparities in violent crime across the city. The analysis transforms raw crime points into meaningful density metrics displayed in an appropriate UTM coordinate system, making patterns immediately visible to policymakers and stakeholders. By comparing the spatial patterns of homicides with Airbnb listings and pricing data, this study illuminates the relationship between public safety perceptions and the tourism economy. The resulting map provides critical insights for resource allocation, community safety initiatives, and understanding how crime geography influences Chicago's visitor industry and neighborhood investment patterns.

Skills Used:
  • Summary Tables - aggregated point data by district to calculate crime totals
  • Joins and Relates - connected tables to combine crime data with police district boundaries
  • Field Calculator - computed density metrics from raw homicide counts
  • Calculate Area - determined square mileage for each police district
  • Coordinate System Transformation - displayed data in appropriate UTM projection
